About This Website

BACKGROUND

Helen Keller International (HKI) has been at the forefront of the development and scaling up of strategies to effectively deliver vitamin A supplementation since the early 1970s. In partnership with government Ministries of Health, UNICEF, Micronutrient Initiative, and other partners, vitamin A supplementation has since become a pillar in all 13 sub-Saharan Africa countries where HKI works. In these countries, vitamin A supplementation is distributed twice-yearly through Child Health Days, in addition to routine distribution.

All countries implementing Child Health Days use tally sheet data to measure coverage, which is the same method used to measure vaccine coverage. This method consistently over-estimates coverage due to inaccurate census data, mathematical errors totaling coverage across districts and regions, and distribution of capsules outside of the target age group.

Since 2011, HKI has conducted over 40 post-event coverage surveys (PECs) in 13 countries to validate coverage reported by tally sheets. The interactive map on this website provides a summary of coverage data from tally sheets and PECS broken down by country and by catchment area. Using the map, coverage can be compared over time and across countries, and the difference between administrative data and PECs validated data can be viewed.

WHAT IS A POST EVENT COVERAGE SURVEY?

A post-event coverage survey (PECs) is a household survey used to verify administrative or tally sheet coverage data following a distribution event or campaign. PECS are also used to evaluate the quality of service delivery and determine factors that affected coverage and knowledge about services distributed. PECs are conducted within six weeks of service distribution in order to ensure accurate recall.

The target groups for PECs are parents and caretakers of children who were eligible to receive services during the distribution. Community leaders, facility-based health workers, distributors, community health workers and other individuals involved in the distribution can also be surveyed as their knowledge and perspectives provide insight into how distribution programs can be strengthened.

PECS methodology is based on the World Health Organization methodology for immunization coverage surveys. In each PECs, 900 caretakers are surveyed from 30 different clusters and asked if their child received the service of interest. The selection of 30 clusters and 30 households per cluster is used to achieve a precise and rapid estimate of service coverage with +/- 5% accuracy.
